User Guide

Construction Calculator
A full construction math calculator with feet-inches support, just like the Construction Master Pro.
Feet-Inches Entry

Type a number, then press Feet to lock it as feet. Type the next number, press Inch for inches.

All math (+, -, ×, ÷) works in decimal feet internally, but the display shows feet-inches too.

Fractions: type the numerator, press /, then the denominator. After locking inches, digits start a fraction directly: 5 Feet 4 Inch 3 / 8 → 5' 4 3/8". Or type 3 / 4 alone for 3/4".

12 Feet 6 Inch + 3 Feet 2 Inch =
= 15' 8"
Pitch / Rise / Run

Enter any two of the three values, and the calculator solves for the third.

Type a number, then press Pitch, Rise, or Run. When two are set, the third appears automatically.

Example: enter 8 Pitch then 12 Run → Rise is solved.

Volume — Length × Width × Height

Enter a dimension and press Length, then the next and press Width, then Height. Two dimensions give the area; the third gives the volume in cubic feet, cubic yards, and 80 lb bags. Dimensions take feet-inches too.

Change one dimension and press its key again to re-figure without starting over.

10' 6" Length 12' Width 8' Height
= 1008 CF   37.33 CY
Material Conversions

Tap Conv to slide open the conversion pad, then convert the number on the display:

Bd Ft — a cubic-foot volume to board feet (×12)

LF — linear feet to how many 8', 12' and 16' sticks to order

Blocks — wall area in SF to 8×16 CMU count (1.125 per SF)

Alongside SF, SY, CY, CF, Acres and the square/cube keys.

Conversions never change your number. The first unit key locks in the calculated value as the base; every conversion after that works from the same base, so you can bounce SY → m² → SY all day. Press the key of the unit on screen to get the original number back exactly. The moment you press a digit or operator, the number you see becomes the working number.

100 SF → Yds 11.11 SY → m 9.29 m² → m
= 100 SF — right back where you started
200 Conv Blocks
= 225 blocks  (press Blocks again for your 200 back)
Memory

Stor — saves current value to memory

Rcl — recalls the stored value

M+ — adds current value to memory (running total)

An M indicator appears in the display when memory has a value.

Switching Calculators

Tap any of the six icons in the header — Calc, Roof, Area, Stair, Conc, Conv — to jump straight to that calculator. The current one is highlighted in gold.

You can also swipe left or right anywhere on the screen to move between them.

Roof Framing
Calculate rafter lengths, angles, and cuts for common rafters, hip/valley at equal or unequal pitches.
Common Rafter

Run — horizontal distance from eave to ridge centerline (in feet)

Pitch — rise per 12 inches of run (e.g., 8 means 8/12 pitch)

Outputs: Rise, Rafter Length, Slope Angle, Plumb Cut, Seat Cut

Run = 12.5 ft, Pitch = 8/12
Rafter Length = 15.02 ft
Hip / Valley

Same Pitch — both sides of a 90° corner have the same pitch. Enter Run and Pitch.

Unequal Pitch — two different pitches meeting at a corner. Enter Run and Pitch for each side (A and B).

Roof Area on Slope
Convert flat (plan view) area to actual sloped area for material ordering. Handles multiple roof sections at different pitches.
How It Works

A sloped roof has more surface area than its footprint. The slope factor = √(1 + (pitch/12)²).

At 8/12 pitch, the slope factor is 1.2019 — meaning 20% more area than flat.

Results include roofing squares (1 square = 100 sq ft) with a 10% waste estimate.

Adding Multiple Areas

Click + Add Area for each roof section. Enter the flat (plan) area in SF and the pitch for each. Different sections can have different pitches.

1000 SF @ 8/12 = 1201.9 sq ft
600 SF @ 6/12 = 670.8 sq ft
Total = 18.73 squares (21 with waste)
Stair Layout
Calculate risers, treads, stringer length, and check IRC code compliance. Supports landings.
Inputs

Total Rise — floor-to-floor height in inches

Target Riser — desired riser height (default 7.5")

Tread Depth — horizontal depth of each tread (default 10")

Headroom — available clearance in inches

Landings

Set Number of Landings to split the stair into flights. Each landing adds its depth to the total run.

The results show a flight-by-flight breakdown with risers, treads, and stringer length per flight.

108" total rise, 7.5" riser, 1 landing
2 flights: 7R/6T + 7R/6T, 13.00 ft span

Multiple Footings

The Footings tab lets you add multiple footing entries. Choose a preset size (16"×8", 20"×10", etc.) or select Custom to enter your own width and depth.

Waste Factor

The 10% waste checkbox adds 10% to the calculated volume. This accounts for spillage, over-excavation, and uneven forms.

Any Unit Converter

The second card converts anything to anything. Type an Amount, pick the unit you have in From, pick what you want in To, and the answer updates as you type.

Five groups: Length (in, ft, yd, mi, mm, cm, m, km), Area (in², ft², yd², acre, m², hectare), Volume (fl oz, cup, pint, quart, gallon, mL, L, in³, ft³, yd³, m³), Weight (oz, lb, ton, g, kg, metric ton) and Temperature.

Pick a From unit and the To list narrows to that group, so you can never ask for gallons in pounds. The ⇆ button swaps the two.

Under the answer it shows the rate for a single unit — handy when you just want to remember that 1 yd³ is 27 ft³.

Mortgage
Buyer payment math, built around one setup you fill in once a day.
Set It Up Once

Open Loan Setup and fill in rate, term, program, down payment, tax basis, insurance and HOA. It saves on the phone and collapses to a one-line chip; Edit reopens it. Every tab then needs only a price or a budget.

Insurance has no default on purpose. Gulf wind premiums run far above what a national calculator assumes, and a guess understates the payment by hundreds a month.

LA Millage mode subtracts the $7,500 homestead exemption from the assessed value (10% of price). That is right for an owner-occupied primary residence — not for a rental or a second home.

Payment — The Whole Check

A buyer sees P&I in an ad — principal and interest, roughly three quarters of what he actually pays. This tab returns PITI: principal, interest, taxes, insurance, mortgage insurance and dues. Quote the gold line.

MI Ends is when mortgage insurance stops. On conventional the app amortizes the loan and finds the month the balance reaches 78% of the price. FHA above 90% LTV reads Life of loan. It is the only part of the payment that shrinks on its own, with no refinance — worth pointing out.

Base Loan and Total Financed differ on FHA and VA, because the upfront MIP or funding fee is rolled in — the buyer borrows more than the house costs.

$285,000 · Conventional · 5% down · 6.5% · 30 yr
105 mills · $2,400 ins · $25 HOA
= $2,212.58/mo   MI ends month 135
Max Price — Budget vs Underwriter

Runs the payment math backward. Use it when a buyer leads with a payment instead of a price. Target Payment is all-in PITI, not just P&I.

Add income and debts and it also works out what debt-to-income allows. Then The Real Ceiling names which one actually held the answer down — and those are two different conversations. If his budget binds, he qualifies for more than he wants to spend, so show him a bigger plan and let him decline. If DTI binds, he wants more than the lender will allow, and walking the floor will not change that.

Budget $1,900 · income $5,000/mo · debts $450 · DTI 45%
DTI binds at $1,800 → max price $227,500
Buydown — One Pot, Three Ways

You have a number you will give up on a house. This tells you how to give it up. The same dollars come back as a closing credit, a permanent rate buydown, and a price cut.

Payment and cash to close pull in opposite directions, so neither settles it. 5-Year Cost does: cash to close + 60 payments − principal paid down. Subtracting principal matters, because a dollar that went to equity is not a dollar he spent. The winner gets a gold border.

Roughly: the credit wins when he is short on cash, the buydown wins when he is short on payment and plans to stay, and the price cut usually buys the least relief per dollar — only part of the cut reaches the loan.

$8,000 incentive on a $285,000 house
Buydown wins: 2.95 pts → 5.761%, $2,083.23/mo
Credit $2,212.58 · Price cut $2,154.95
Payoff — What Extra Principal Buys

For when a buyer asks what an extra $200 a month really does. Enter the loan balance (not the sale price) and the extra; rate and term come from Loan Setup. The app walks the loan month by month until the balance hits zero.

This is principal and interest only. Taxes and insurance are escrow, not loan balance, so extra principal does not shorten them. The payment here is smaller than the PITI on the Payment tab, and that is correct.

$270,750 balance · 6.5% · 30 yr · $200 extra
Paid off in 22 yr 6 mo — saves $100,210
Good to Know

Millage is the whole number (105), not a percent. Down payment is entered as a percent only — convert a flat dollar amount yourself.

The PMI override applies to conventional only; FHA and VA use their own published factors and ignore it. Left blank, PMI is estimated from a 740+ credit grid.

The app does not enforce program minimums — it will run FHA at 1% down without complaint, so enter a real down payment.

Results show the down payment but not full cash to close: no closing costs, prepaids or escrow funding. The Buydown tab is where cash to close appears.

Loan Setup lives on this one phone. The collapsed chip is the only sign of what is loaded — read it before quoting, or yesterday's VA buyer will price today's conventional deal.

Business & Financing
The second card on the Money screen: job pricing, equipment loans, and draw requests.
Markup Is Not Margin

Markup is profit as a percent of cost. Margin is the same dollars as a percent of price. Different denominators, so they are never the same number — 25% markup is 20% margin. Both rows print on every result so you cannot quote one thinking it is the other.

That is not academic. On $1,754.80 of cost, multiplying by 1.25 gives $2,193.50 — a 20% margin. If 25% margin is what the job needed, the price is $2,339.73. Repeat that across every option in a spec house and it is real money.

Burden is everything riding on the wage — payroll tax, comp, liability, truck — so 35 means the hour costs 1.35 times the rate. To price forward, use Target Margin. To check a price already on paper, use Or Quoted Price; profit turns red if it is under cost.

16 hr · $28/hr · 35% burden · $1,150 material · 20% margin
Cost $1,754.80
Price $2,193.50 — 25.00% markup / 20.00% margin
Loan — Leave One Blank

Truck, skid steer, trailer, any flat installment loan. Fill in three of Amount, Rate, Term and Payment, leave the fourth empty, and it fills the hole. The solved row comes back highlighted.

The rate case earns its keep. A dealer quotes a payment, not a rate — "$899 a month, 60 months, $45,000." Type those three, blank the rate, and you get the rate buried in that payment to hold against what your bank would write.

Interest as % of Amount is over the whole life of the loan, not a yearly rate — do not line it up against the APR.

$45,000 · 60 months · $899/mo · rate blank
= 7.373% APR — $8,940.00 total interest
Draw — AIA G702 Order

Bill the owner or the bank for work in place, in the order a lender's inspector expects: completed to date, plus stored materials, minus retainage held, minus what you have already been paid.

Enter Work Complete as the percentage of the whole contract finished to date — not what you added since the last draw.

Here is the part that costs money. Previously Paid must be cash actually received, net of retainage — not the gross you billed. Enter gross and you subtract the old retainage twice and under-request by exactly what is already being held. A negative Payment Due turns red, and that is almost always this mistake.

On the last application tick Final draw to release everything withheld over the life of the job.

$185,000 contract · 65% complete · $4,500 stored
10% retainage · $99,000 previously paid (net)
= $13,275.00 due this draw
Enter $110,000 gross instead and it reads $2,275 — $11,000 short.
